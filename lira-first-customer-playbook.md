# How to Get Your First Customer with Zero Proof
### The Exact Playbook for Lira Before a Single Case Study Exists

---

## The Honest Truth First

You are not in a weak position. You are in a **different** position.

Every company that exists today — Gong, HubSpot, Intercom, Stripe, Salesforce — had this exact same moment. Zero customers. Zero case studies. Zero logos. Just a product and a belief that it solved a real problem.

Here is how they got through it:

- **Stripe**: Patrick and John Collison would literally sit next to early customers and say *"let me get you set up right now"* — they personally installed Stripe in real time during the conversation. They did not wait to be ready.
- **Airbnb**: Brian Chesky personally visited every New York host, took photos himself, and made every listing look beautiful. He did the work manually so he could understand whether the value was real.
- **Gong**: Amit Bendov (CEO) did every single early demo himself. No sales team. Just the founder with unshakeable belief in the product, talking to anyone who would listen.
- **HubSpot**: Dharmesh Shah gave away Website Grader (a free tool) to establish credibility before anyone paid a dollar.
- **Dropbox**: Drew Houston made a demo video of a product that wasn't fully built yet — just to see if people wanted it. 70,000 people signed up overnight.

The pattern across all of them is the same:

> **The first customers didn't buy a proven product. They said yes to a founder who understood their problem better than they did.**

That is your actual competitive advantage right now. You understand the BFSI customer support problem deeply — you've researched it, built a product spec around it, and know the exact pain (payment failures, KYC delays, Intercom's broken pricing model). Most vendors can't articulate that pain at the level you can.

## The Reframe That Changes Everything

Stop thinking of yourself as trying to **sell** to a first customer.

Start thinking of yourself as looking for a **design partner**.

These are not the same thing:

| Selling to a Customer | Recruiting a Design Partner |
|---|---|
| "Here's our product — will you buy it?" | "We're building something specifically for BFSI CX teams and want to build it *with* one company, not just for them" |
| They evaluate you against alternatives | They get something no one else has — exclusive early access and direct influence on the product |
| You need proof points to win | They understand they **are** the proof point — that's the deal |
| Pressure on both sides | Genuine partnership |
| Full commercial expectations | Free or deeply discounted for the first 6 months |

**The offer:** *"We're offering 3 Founding Partner spots. You get Lira free for 6 months, a dedicated setup, and your team has direct access to our engineering. In exchange, we ask for weekly feedback calls and an honest case study if the results are there."*

That is not charity. That is a genuinely valuable offer. They get:
- A product built around their specific pain
- 6 months free (real monetary value)
- The ability to say they were early and shaped it
- Direct access to the founders

You get:
- Real usage data
- A documented case study
- A logo you can show
- Honest product feedback that makes everything better
- A reference you can name-drop in every future sales conversation

## Step 1: The Warm Network Audit — Do This Before Any Cold Outreach

The single fastest path to a first customer is **not the 100-company cold list.** It is someone who already has a reason to give you the benefit of the doubt.

Do this exercise right now. Open your phone contacts, WhatsApp, LinkedIn connections, and Gmail. Ask honestly:

**Who do I know — even loosely — who:**
- Works at a fintech, payment company, lending platform, crypto company, or any BFSI startup?
- Is a founder, co-founder, or early employee at such a company?
- Has complained about customer support, Intercom, Zendesk, or support costs in my presence?
- Works in a customer-facing role (CX, Customer Success, Operations) anywhere?
- Went to the same university / bootcamp / accelerator and is now in a relevant company?
- Is a former colleague, client, or professional contact from a previous role?

Write every name down. Even if the connection feels thin. A former classmate who now works at a payments startup is NOT a cold lead. They are a warm conversation waiting to happen.

**The ask for a warm contact:**
Not: *"Can you be a customer?"*

> *"Hey [Name], you might have seen I've been building Lira — AI customer support specifically for fintech. I'm at the stage where I'm looking for 2–3 companies to pilot with, for free, in exchange for honest feedback. I thought of you because [specific reason — they work in the right space, they've complained about support, etc.]. Even if it's not a fit, would you know anyone in your network who lives with this problem daily? Happy to jump on a quick call."*

This works for five reasons:
1. It's honest about where you are (early, looking for pilots, free)
2. It asks for help, not money — people's defenses drop
3. It includes a fallback ask (do you know anyone?) — almost everyone can answer that
4. It doesn't require them to believe in your product yet — just to make an introduction
5. The word "free" removes all objections about risk

## Step 2: The 5 Most Reliable Paths to a First Customer

In order of likelihood to actually work for Lira right now:

### Path 1: Your Immediate Network → Design Partner (Fastest)
As described above. Audit your warm contacts. Reach out to 10–15 relevant people with the design partner offer. Statistically, 1–3 of them will say yes or refer someone who will.

### Path 2: The Public Complaint → Direct Offer (Fastest for finding motivated buyers)
Go to Twitter/X right now. Search: `"Intercom" "charging" OR "expensive" OR "not working"` and `"fintech" OR "payments" OR "KYC"`.

Read carefully. When you find someone genuinely frustrated, look at their profile. If they're at a company in your ICP — send this:

> *"Saw your post about [specific complaint]. We're building something specifically for this — Lira AI for BFSI support. We're looking for a small number of companies to pilot with, completely free, and what you described is exactly the use case we designed for. Would 15 minutes be worth your time to see if we can help?"*

They were already expressing the pain publicly. You are not cold-calling — you are responding to a request for help they didn't know they were making.

### Path 3: The LinkedIn Build-in-Public → Inbound Pull (Takes 3–6 weeks but scales)
Start posting on LinkedIn *right now* — even before you have customers. The format:

- *"We're building Lira — AI customer support for fintech. Here's a problem we keep hearing from BFSI teams: [specific pain]. We built [specific feature] to solve it. Here's how it works."*
- *"Day 14 of building Lira. Spoke to 8 fintech CX leaders this week. The most common thing I heard: [direct quote or paraphrase]. Our answer to this is [specific thing]."*
- *"Unpopular opinion: Every AI support tool is just a fancy FAQ bot. Here's what actually needs to be different."*

What happens: people in your ICP who are silently experiencing the pain read your post, recognize themselves in it, and DM you. These inbound DMs convert at a dramatically higher rate than any outbound message — because they came to you.

This takes weeks to build momentum but creates a compounding asset that works while you sleep.

### Path 4: The Fintech Accelerator / Community Warm Introduction (High Trust, Low Friction)
Every fintech accelerator (YCombinator, Techstars, Founders Factory, Catalyst Fund, Flat6Labs, Founders Factory Africa) has a community of portfolio companies who are:
- Growing fast (support problems compound with growth)
- Trust signal from the accelerator ("they backed this, so it's credible")
- Accessible through the accelerator network

**The move:** Contact the program manager or community manager of a relevant fintech accelerator. Say:

> *"I'm building Lira — AI customer support for BFSI companies. We're offering 3 free pilot spots for portfolio companies experiencing support scaling pain. Would this be something you'd share with your community, or introduce me to 2–3 companies it might fit?"*

One warm intro from an accelerator network manager reaches 30–50 relevant companies. And because they vouched for you, the conversation starts at a completely different trust level.

### Path 5: The Conference Talk / Panel Slot → Visible Authority (Takes time but compounds)
Apply to speak at fintech events as a subject matter expert on AI customer support — not as a vendor pitching Lira. Proposed talks:
- *"Why fintech AI support tools fail at the worst moment — and what the architecture needs to look like"*
- *"From reactive to proactive: the next evolution of customer support in BFSI"*
- *"How fintech companies are losing customers silently — and what to do about it"*

You don't need customers to have credibility on this topic. You have the research, the data, and the product vision. One talk at a fintech conference puts you in front of 50–200 relevant people who self-selected as interested in this problem.

## Step 3: What to Say When They Ask "Do You Have Any Customers?"

This question will come. Be ready. The worst answers are:
- ❌ Deflecting: *"We're in early access..."*
- ❌ Lying: *"Yes, we work with several companies..."* (Never do this)
- ❌ Apologizing: *"We're very early, so we don't have case studies yet, but..."*

The honest, confident answer that actually works:

> *"We don't have live customer case studies yet — we're deliberately selecting our first 3 founding partners right now. The reason we're selective is because our founding partners get something no future customer gets: direct access to our engineering team, 6 months free, and meaningful influence on the product roadmap. We've built this specific to BFSI support problems — payment failures, KYC delays, escalation routing — because that's where the gap is clearest. You'd be the first, which is actually the best position to be in."*

Then immediately flip it:

> *"Can I ask — what does your current support escalation rate look like? I want to understand if the specific problem we've built for is actually your problem."*

This answer works because:
1. It's completely honest — no pretending
2. It frames "no customers" as a feature, not a bug — because design partners genuinely do get something better
3. It pivots immediately to their pain — which is more interesting to them than your proof anyway
4. It radiates confidence — you're not apologizing for being early

## Step 4: The Free Pilot Structure — How to Set It Up So It Produces a Case Study

A free pilot with no structure produces nothing useful. A structured free pilot produces a case study, a testimonial, and a reference.

### The Founding Partner Agreement (What You Ask For — Verbally or in a Simple Email)

**What they get:**
- Lira fully set up for their support use case (you do the implementation)
- 6 months free
- Direct Slack channel with your founding team
- Priority feature requests — 2 features of their choice added to the roadmap with a delivery commitment

**What you ask for:**
- 1 x 30-minute call per week for the first month (decreases to monthly after that)
- Honest feedback — if it's not working, tell us directly
- If the results are real after 90 days: one documented case study (we write it, you approve it)
- If the results are real after 90 days: one 15-minute reference call for future prospects who ask

**The case study format you want:**
1. Company name and what they do (1 sentence)
2. What their support problem was before Lira (specific — escalation rate, ticket volume, tool cost)
3. Exactly how Lira was set up for their use case
4. The result after 90 days (specific numbers — escalation rate changed from X to Y, first response time changed from X to Y, support cost changed)
5. A direct quote from the Head of CX or founder
6. Permission to use their logo

**The result that makes future sales infinitely easier:**
> *"[Company Name], a 150-person Nigerian payments platform, reduced their support escalation rate from 42% to 17% in 90 days using Lira. Their first response time dropped from 4 hours to 28 seconds. They eliminated 2 planned support agent hires, saving ~$45,000/year."*

One sentence like that, with a named company and verifiable numbers, changes every future sales conversation forever.

## Step 5: Do Things That Don't Scale (The Stripe Rule)

This is the most important advice Paul Graham ever gave, and it applies directly to you right now.

Stripe didn't wait for their payment product to be perfect before getting customers. They personally installed it for people. Manually. One by one.

For Lira's first customers: **if the automated product isn't ready for a specific use case, do it manually first.**

If a design partner says "we need Lira to handle KYC delay notifications specifically" — and that exact flow isn't automated yet — do it manually for them while you build the automation. Set up a webhook, write the email yourself, send it through Resend manually if needed. The customer experiences the value. You learn exactly what to build.

This is not cheating. This is how every great product gets built. You find out what people actually need by delivering it to them, even imperfectly, before you invest in automating the wrong thing.

## The Single Most Important Thing to Remember

You do not need a perfect product or a track record to get your first customer.

You need one person to believe that **you understand their exact problem** better than anyone else they've talked to — and that you are determined to solve it.

That belief comes from how specifically you describe their world back to them. When you say:

> *"You're running a payments platform, your KYC process occasionally blocks customers at the worst moment — right after they've committed to a transaction — and your support team finds out about it from an angry email, hours after the customer has already lost trust in your product."*

...and they say "yes, exactly" — that is the moment the first sale begins.

Not because of your product. Because of your understanding.

Build the understanding. The proof will follow.
