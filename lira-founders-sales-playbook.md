# How the Best B2B SaaS Companies Got Their First Customers
### Real Stories from Founders Who Built Billion-Dollar Companies from Zero

> *"Do things that don't scale."*
> — Paul Graham, Y Combinator

## Introduction: The Counter-Intuitive Truth About Getting Started

Every founder searches for the scalable playbook. The automated funnel. The growth hack. The one thing that goes viral.

Here is what the data actually shows, across every B2B SaaS company that crossed $10M ARR:

**The first 10 customers were never won by scale. They were won by obsession.**

Obsession with the customer's problem. Obsession with proving the model. Obsession with being present, personal, and impossible to ignore.

This document is a collection of the exact tactics, stories, and frameworks from the founders and revenue leaders who have proven it — applied directly to where Lira is today.

## The Stories

### Story 1: Stripe — The Collison Installation
**Patrick & John Collison | Outcome: $95 billion valuation**

In 2009, Stripe was a payments API that nobody had heard of. Every other payment company had a complex sign-up process, lengthy documentation, and weeks of integration time.

Patrick and John Collison did something so simple it became legendary: **they would set up Stripe for you on the spot, in real time, as you watched.**

Early Y Combinator founders called this the "Collison Installation." If you expressed even mild interest in Stripe, Patrick would say: *"Give me your laptop."* He'd have you live on Stripe's API in 15 minutes, before the skepticism could calcify.

**What they knew that most people miss:**
- The biggest conversion killer is not pricing — it is effort
- If you eliminate all friction between "interested" and "using it," conversion becomes almost inevitable
- The first version doesn't need to scale — it needs to prove that people want the thing

**The principle:** When you have a product someone needs, the fastest path to proof is to remove all barriers between them and using it. Do it manually. Do it personally. Do it right now.

**Applied to Lira:** When a prospect expresses interest, offer to set up their first knowledge base, connect their first integration, and run a live demo using their actual support queue — not a fake scenario. Make the first experience of Lira feel effortless and immediate.

### Story 2: Slack — Give It to Your Friends First
**Stewart Butterfield | Outcome: $27.7 billion acquisition by Salesforce**

Slack wasn't built as a product. It was built as an internal communication tool for Tiny Speck, the game company building Glitch. When Glitch failed, Stewart Butterfield turned their internal tool into a product.

For the first customers: he called his friends who ran companies he respected — Rdio (music startup), Cozy (real estate) — and said: *"Try this. It's free. Tell me what you think."*

He chose people he trusted to give honest feedback. He did not choose the earliest, most desperate adopters — he chose the ones whose opinion he actually respected. Their feedback shaped every early product decision.

Then came the real genius: **he made each new customer onboarding feel like a personal invitation.** Early Slack onboarding emails felt hand-written. Early support responses felt like a friend helping you, not a ticket system.

By the time Slack launched publicly in August 2013, it had 8,000 users in 24 hours — but that wasn't magic. It was the product of thousands of hours of personal, high-touch early relationships that built word of mouth before launch.

**The principle:** Your first customers are not sources of revenue. They are co-developers and marketing assets. Choose them deliberately. Over-invest in making them successful. Their word is worth more than any ad.

**Applied to Lira:** The 3 design partners you recruit are not just pilots. They're the origin story. They will tell 10 colleagues. Their names in your case study will get 10 more companies to take a call. Their logos are your first credibility. Choose them as carefully as you choose a co-founder.

### Story 3: HubSpot — Build the Audience Before the Product
**Dharmesh Shah & Brian Halligan | Outcome: $19B+ public company**

In 2004, before HubSpot had a single paying customer, Dharmesh Shah wrote a blog post titled *"Don't Think Outside the Box — Think Like There Is No Box."* It got 50,000 visitors.

That blog became what eventually became HubSpot. By the time HubSpot launched its product, Dharmesh and Brian had:
- A widely-read blog on inbound marketing (their invented concept)
- A free tool: Website Grader — which graded any website's SEO and marketing. 4 million websites were graded.
- Speaking slots at conferences explaining the concept of "inbound marketing" as a category
- A $9.99 book ("Inbound Marketing") that sold 50,000+ copies

None of this was about HubSpot the software. All of it was about building trust with the exact buyer who would eventually pay for HubSpot the software.

When HubSpot eventually sold its product, it was not selling to cold strangers. It was selling to **an audience that already trusted them as the authority on the topic.**

**The principle:** If you build an audience around the problem before you sell the solution, you never have to do cold outreach at scale. The demand comes to you.

**Applied to Lira:** The LinkedIn content strategy is not a marketing activity — it is the foundation of the sales motion. Every post about "why fintech support tools fail" is building an audience of people who will, eventually, ask to see Lira. Your content is your first sales team. Hire it now.

### Story 4: Gong — Cold Call Every VP of Sales in Silicon Valley
**Amit Bendov (CEO) | Outcome: $7.5B valuation, $298M ARR**

Gong had no customers, no case studies, and no proof in May 2016 when Amit Bendov started selling. What he had was a thesis: that sales leaders were flying blind because they had no data on what actually happened on sales calls.

His strategy was brutally simple and almost offensively manual: he personally cold-called every VP of Sales at a Bay Area technology company. Not an SDR. Not a sequence tool. Amit himself.

He called somewhere between 200–400 people in the first few months.

His pitch wasn't about Gong. It was about a specific problem he understood in detail: *"You're coaching your reps based on the 1 call you happened to sit in on per month. The 499 other calls are invisible to you. What if you could see everything?"*

He got on calls. He listened. He let them describe the problem in their own words. He did not pitch until they had told him exactly why they needed what he was building.

Amit's other secret: **Gong used Gong on every single sales call from day one.** Every prospect call was recorded and analyzed by the product. Amit knew exactly which talking points worked, which questions landed, and which moments caused the deal to move. He coached himself using the product he was selling.

**The principle 1:** The founder is the best first salesperson. Nobody knows the problem or the product like you do. Don't outsource the first 50 sales conversations to someone else.

**The principle 2:** Use your own product. If you wouldn't eat your own cooking, why should your customer?

**Applied to Lira:** Every meeting you take to sell Lira should have Lira in it. Every sales call, every discovery conversation, every demo — Lira is present, recording, generating the follow-up. Your prospects will notice. That is your demo.

### Story 5: Salesforce — Pick a Fight with an Entire Industry
**Marc Benioff | Outcome: $250B+ valuation, invented modern SaaS**

In 1999, Siebel Systems dominated enterprise CRM. Oracle and SAP owned the software market. Nobody in their right mind was challenging them.

Marc Benioff launched Salesforce with a campaign called **"The End of Software."** He printed thousands of red circles with a line through the word "Software." He put them on t-shirts, billboards, and handouts. He **paid protesters to march outside the Siebel user conference.**

He organized "No Software" parties at competitor conferences. He created a fake protestor organization that was actually run by Salesforce marketing.

Was it ridiculous? Yes. Did it work? The company went public in 2004. They are now larger than all of those competitors combined.

The insight: **in a crowded market, conventional marketing makes you invisible. Being outrageous makes you remembered.**

Marc also gave Salesforce away for free to small businesses for six months. Not a trial — free. The logic: if we can prove value to a small business in six months, they'll pay anything to keep it, and their reference will reach the enterprise.

**The principle:** You can define a new category with a bold, clear, contrarian position. If the positioning is specific and the emotion is strong enough, people will argue about it on your behalf. That argument is free marketing.

**Applied to Lira:** Your contrarian position already exists: *"Every AI support tool is a reactive inbox with a chatbot bolted on. Lira is the only one built proactively — at the architecture level."* Say it boldly. Say it publicly. Let Intercom fans argue with you. That argument is more valuable than any ad.

### Story 6: Zoom — Win Every Customer, Lose None
**Eric Yuan (CEO) | Outcome: $100B+ peak valuation**

Eric Yuan left Cisco's WebEx in 2011 with 40 engineers to build Zoom. His obsession was almost pathological about one thing: **customer happiness.**

In the early days, if a Zoom customer was unhappy, Eric Yuan personally emailed them. Not his team. Eric himself. He would ask: *"What did we do wrong?"* And then he'd fix it.

Zoom offered free refunds without question. If a customer was dissatisfied, they got their money back no questions asked — even for reasons that weren't Zoom's fault.

Eric's thesis: *"In the early days, your customer success rate is your marketing budget."* Happy customers tell people. Unhappy customers leave and say nothing, which is worse.

He targeted a specific segment first: education. Universities were poorly served by existing video conferencing tools. Zoom gave free accounts to Stanford professors and students. Stanford students graduated and went to companies. Those companies then heard about Zoom through their employees who had grown up using it.

**The principle:** In SaaS, customer success is a growth strategy. Your first customers' satisfaction rate is more important than your first customers' deal size.

**Applied to Lira:** When you get your first design partner, over-deliver so completely that they become a reference you can use forever. A 5-star testimonial from a named fintech company, given in year one, will generate more revenue than any marketing spend.

### Story 7: Intercom — Write the Thing Before You Build the Thing
**Des Traynor, Eoghan McCabe, Ciaran Lee, David Barrett | Outcome: $1.3B valuation**

Intercom launched in 2011. Before they had a product, they had Des Traynor's blog.

Des wrote about the future of customer communication with a clarity and specificity that nobody else had. People shared his posts widely because they articulated a problem in a way that felt like he had read their minds.

By the time Intercom launched, they had an audience who was already convinced of the problem. The product was almost secondary — people signed up because they trusted Des's thinking.

Intercom's early growth was almost entirely content-led. Their blog attracted thousands of readers per week in the earliest months. Those readers became users. Those users became the testimonials.

**The tactical lesson:** The blog post that articulates your customer's pain more accurately than they can articulate it themselves is worth more than a landing page.

**Applied to Lira:** Write one blog post titled something like: *"Why every AI support bot has a 30% real resolution rate — and what the actual number means for fintech revenue."* Make it data-backed. Make it uncomfortable. Make it the most honest thing anyone in the industry has written. That post is a lead generation engine.

### Story 8: Dropbox — Prove Demand Before You Build
**Drew Houston | Outcome: $7.2B market cap at IPO**

In 2007, Dropbox did not have a working product. Drew Houston had the idea, had some code, but the product was not ready.

He recorded a 3-minute screencast showing what Dropbox *would* do. The video was casual, funny, aimed at Hacker News' sensibility. He posted it.

Overnight, 70,000 people signed up for the waitlist.

The product did not exist. The demand was proven before the product was finished. That demand gave Drew the confidence and the investor validation to actually build the thing properly.

**The principle:** You do not need to build the full product to prove demand. You need to articulate the value clearly enough that people want it. That proof of demand is more valuable than any product.

**Applied to Lira:** A 2-minute Loom video showing the proactive payment failure scenario — even if it's partially manual behind the scenes — sent to 10 fintech VPs of CX, is a legitimate demand-proof exercise. Their reaction tells you more than months of market research.

### Story 9: Notion — Community-Led Growth
**Ivan Zhao | Outcome: $10B valuation**

Notion launched in 2018 to a small audience that had been building things in Notion's beta. The growth acceleration came from one place: Reddit.

Ivan Zhao and the team discovered that users were obsessively sharing Notion templates on Reddit — in r/productivity, r/getmotivated, r/gtd. These were not power users; they were regular people who had built something useful in Notion and wanted to share it.

The team made a decision: lean into the community. They responded to every template share. They featured community members. They made the community feel like they were part of the product.

The result: Notion's growth from 2018–2020 was almost entirely community-driven. No paid ads. No outbound sales team. Just a passionate user base sharing what they had built.

**The principle:** If your users are creating value with your product, make it easy for them to share that value. The community that forms around a product is often more powerful than the product itself.

**Applied to Lira:** Even at the design partner stage, if your first customers are getting results — make sharing easy. Draft the LinkedIn post for them. Write the case study. Create the template. Make it a 5-minute action, not a 5-hour one.

## The Framework: What All These Stories Have in Common

Across every one of these companies, seven patterns repeat:

### Pattern 1: The Founder Sold First
In every case, the person most obsessed with the problem — the founder — did the first sales personally. Not because they had a sales background. Because their obsession was the credibility. Nobody knows the problem better than the person who devoted years to solving it.

### Pattern 2: They Targeted One Segment Precisely
Gong didn't target "all companies." Zoom didn't target "all businesses." Intercom didn't target "all companies with customers." Each one picked a beachhead:
- Gong → VP Sales at Bay Area tech companies
- Zoom → University professors
- HubSpot → SMB marketing managers
- Stripe → YC-backed startups

The narrower the target, the faster the proof. Once you have proof in one segment, expansion becomes credible.

### Pattern 3: They Did Things That Don't Scale — On Purpose
The Collison Installation couldn't scale. Marc Benioff personally organizing fake protests couldn't scale. Amit Bendov making 400 personal calls couldn't scale.

But they didn't need to scale yet. They needed to **prove the model.** Scale came after proof. Proof came from doing things manually that later became automated.

### Pattern 4: They Used Content to Build Audiences Before Asking for Money
HubSpot, Intercom, Dropbox — all built audiences first. They gave away knowledge, tools, or insights that were genuinely valuable. When they eventually asked for money, they were asking people who already trusted them.

### Pattern 5: They Made Success Easy to Share
Notion templates. Dropbox file sharing. Slack's invite mechanism. Gong call snippets shared in Slack. Every one of these products had a moment where using it created a natural shareable output.

### Pattern 6: They Were Honest About Being Early
None of these companies pretended to be bigger than they were. Stripe told YC founders they were early. Slack told early users they were building it together. This honesty built trust. And trust accelerated adoption more than any polished pitch deck.

### Pattern 7: They Defined a New Category
Salesforce invented "SaaS." HubSpot invented "inbound marketing." Gong invented "conversation intelligence." When you name the category, you own it. Your competitors forever live in your shadow.

## The Lira Application: Your First 90 Days

### Month 1: Prove Demand
- Identify your 10 warmest contacts in fintech/BFSI
- Send them a 2-minute Loom of one specific scenario (payment failure proactive support)
- Track who watches it all the way through — those are your design partner candidates
- Have 5 genuine discovery conversations using SPIN questions — record them (with permission)
- Post 3x/week on LinkedIn about the problem — not the solution yet

### Month 2: Land One Design Partner
- Convert the most excited prospect from Month 1 to a formal design partner
- Set them up — personally, manually — with complete white-glove onboarding
- Use Lira in every sales call you make for Lira (meta-proof)
- Begin building the case study structure from day one — measure baseline before Lira goes live

### Month 3: Generate the First Proof Moment
- By day 60, you should have one specific measurable result from the design partner
- Even a small number is powerful: *"First response time dropped from 6 hours to 4 minutes"*
- Turn that into a post, a case study page, and a reference to use in every sales conversation
- That one result is now your answer to every "do you have any customers?" question forever

## The Most Important Mindset Shift

Every founder who succeeded at this did so by accepting one uncomfortable truth:

**The first 90 days are not about building revenue. They are about building proof.**

Revenue without proof is unsustainable. Proof without revenue is just expensive. But proof — a real result at a real company that you can name — unlocks everything:
- Every future sales conversation starts differently
- Investors take you more seriously
- Partners return your emails
- Prospects believe you are not just another AI company

One named case study with a real, specific result is the most valuable asset Lira can create in the next 90 days. Everything else is secondary.

Go get it.
