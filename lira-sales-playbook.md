## Introduction: The Shift That Changed Everything

In 2026, the single biggest mistake a salesperson can make is to walk in and pitch.

The old model was:
> **Find lead → Cold call → Pitch → Overcome objections → Close → Repeat**

The modern model is:
> **Build presence → Educate → Create demand → Qualify with precision → Earn trust → Close with certainty**

The difference is profound. In the old model, the seller holds all the information and the buyer is skeptical. In the new model, the buyer has already done 70% of their research before they ever speak to you — your job is to be the resource they find during that research.

This document is your complete sales education — grounded in the best methodologies from the greatest salespeople in history, and applied directly to selling Lira to BFSI companies.

# PART 2: THE METHODOLOGY
## The Sales Frameworks That Built Empires

These are not theories. These are the frameworks used at Salesforce, HubSpot, Oracle, and every top-performing SaaS company in the world.

---

### Framework 1: The Challenger Sale (Matthew Dixon & Brent Adamson)
**Source:** CEB/Gartner research — studied 6,000+ salespeople over 5 years

This is the single most validated sales methodology of the last 20 years.

The research found that top performers don't just respond to customer needs — they **challenge** their customer's thinking. They:

1. **Teach** — Share an insight the customer doesn't know that reframes the problem
2. **Tailor** — Customize the insight to their specific industry/role
3. **Take Control** — Guide the commercial conversation confidently

**Applied to Lira:**

Do NOT open with: *"Hi, we're Lira, an AI company that does customer support."*

Open with a **commercial teach**:
> *"Companies in fintech are losing customers at a rate they don't fully see yet. Zendesk/Intercom tells them how many tickets they closed. It doesn't tell them how many customers went quiet after a bad experience and cancelled next quarter. We've analyzed 30 BFSI companies and found that proactive support — reaching customers before they complain — reduces churn by 23%. I'd love to show you how that applies specifically to [Company Name]."*

The insight comes first. The pitch comes last, if at all.

---

### Framework 2: SPIN Selling (Neil Rackham)
**Source:** Huthwaite International — 35,000 sales calls studied

SPIN is how you structure a discovery call. Each letter is a question type:

**S — Situation Questions** *(understand where they are now)*
- "How are you currently handling your inbound customer support?"
- "What tools are you using — Intercom, Zendesk, something else?"
- "How many support agents do you have compared to active customers?"

**P — Problem Questions** *(surface the pain)*
- "When a payment fails on your platform, what happens next in your process?"
- "How often do customers reach out about issues that could have been resolved proactively?"
- "What does your escalation rate look like? What percentage of tickets need a human?"

**I — Implication Questions** *(make the pain feel expensive)* ← THIS IS THE GOLD
- "If 20% of those escalations are people who then churn because they felt poorly served — what's that worth in ARR?"
- "If your support agents are spending 60% of their time on questions an AI should handle — how much is that costing you in salaries right now?"
- "How does that CSAT score affect your ability to expand into enterprise markets?"

**N — Need-Payoff Questions** *(get them to describe the solution)*
- "If your support system could automatically detect a failed payment and reach out to the customer before they even noticed — how would that change your churn rate?"
- "If you could cut first response time from 4 hours to 30 seconds while reducing your support headcount — what would that free you to do?"

**The rule:** Never propose a solution until they have felt the pain in their own words. The implication questions are where the deal is won or lost.

---

### Framework 3: Gap Selling (Keenan)
**Source:** ASG Group — the modern update to SPIN

Keenan's framework says the entire sale lives in the **gap** between where the customer is now (Current State) and where they want to be (Future State).

Your job is not to pitch features. Your job is to:
1. **Define their Current State with specificity** (not "you have a support problem" — "your average first response time is 6 hours, you have a 34% escalation rate, and you're paying $2,400/month for Intercom")
2. **Paint a Future State they emotionally want** ("what if Lira handled 70% of tickets autonomously, your first response was 28 seconds, and your support cost per resolution dropped from $8 to $0.10?")
3. **Show Lira as the bridge** — not through features, through outcomes

**The Gap Formula:**
> *Current pain = Future aspiration − Present reality*

Every conversation, every email, every demo should be built around this gap.

---

### Framework 4: Sandler Selling System (David Sandler)
**Source:** Sandler Training — 50+ years of enterprise sales data

Sandler's core insight: **Most salespeople try to close too early and qualify too late.**

Sandler reverses the traditional funnel. His rules:

1. **Qualify brutally first.** If they don't have the pain, the budget, and the decision-making authority — end the conversation early. Time is your only non-renewable resource.
2. **Let the prospect sell themselves.** Use pain questions so devastating that they are describing Lira's solution before you've even mentioned Lira. "So what you're saying is, you need a system that could automatically detect that issue and handle it without your team intervening?" "Yes." "That's exactly what we do."
3. **Budget before demo.** Never give a product demo to someone who hasn't confirmed budget. The demo is a closing tool, not a discovery tool.
4. **Pain → Money → Decision** — in that order. Never skip steps.

**The Sandler Pain Funnel** (use these questions in order):
1. "Tell me about the problem."
2. "How long has this been an issue?"
3. "What have you tried to fix it?"
4. "Why didn't that work?"
5. "What happens if you don't solve this in the next 12 months?"
6. "How does this affect you personally?"
7. "Have you thought about what solving this would be worth to you?"

By question 7, they are emotionally ready to hear a solution.

---

### Framework 5: The Predictable Revenue Model (Aaron Ross)
**Source:** Salesforce's original outbound sales machine — the model that built Salesforce from $1M to $100M ARR

Aaron Ross separated the SDR (prospecting) function from the AE (closing) function. This is the framework every top SaaS company runs:

**The Three Roles (even if you're a team of 2 right now):**
1. **Seeds** — Inbound leads from marketing content, SEO, word of mouth
2. **Nets** — Campain-level outbound (email sequences, LinkedIn campaigns)
3. **Spears** — Highly personalized, human-led outreach to named accounts (your 100-company list)

**For Lira right now:** You are doing Spears. The 100 companies on your list are named target accounts. Each one gets a customized, personalized outreach — not a blast.

**The key rule:** A "Spear" is not a template email. It is a message that proves you researched this specific company and have a specific insight relevant to them.

---

### Framework 6: The Mom Test (Rob Fitzpatrick)
**Source:** Not traditionally a "sales" book — but critical for early-stage

This applies to your discovery calls. The rule: **Never ask someone if they like your product.** They will lie.

Instead, ask about their life:
- "Walk me through the last time a customer contacted you about a payment problem."
- "What did your support team do? How long did that take?"
- "What's the worst support experience you've had in the last 6 months?"
- "Are you actively looking for better solutions, or is this a future thing?"

**The answers tell you more than any feature validation survey ever could.**

# PART 3: THE FUNNEL
## How Modern B2B Sales Actually Works

Modern B2B sales is not a pipeline. It is a **trust-building system** with multiple parallel tracks.

```
                    AWARENESS
              (They discover you exist)
                        ↓
                    INTEREST
          (They believe you understand their world)
                        ↓
                   CONSIDERATION
          (They compare you to alternatives)
                        ↓
                     INTENT
           (They are actively evaluating you)
                        ↓
                   EVALUATION
              (Demo, trial, proof of value)
                        ↓
                    PURCHASE
               (Contract → Onboarding)
                        ↓
                    EXPANSION
            (Land small → expand wide)
```

The critical insight: **Your marketing must do the Awareness and Interest work. Your sales motion starts at Consideration.**

If your sales team is introducing your company at the Awareness stage, you are paying salespeople to do marketing. That is inefficient and expensive.

> *"Hi [Name], I'm [Name] from [Company]. We help companies like yours improve their customer experience with AI. We work with [generic logos]. Would you be open to a 15-minute call?"*

This email is dead on arrival. It is about the sender. It contains no proof of research. It provides no value. It insults the reader's intelligence.

### The Anatomy of a Cold Email That Gets Replies

Fortune 500 sales teams (specifically the top SDR teams at Salesforce, HubSpot, and Gong) follow this structure:

```
LINE 1: Prove you did your homework (specific, personal, researched)
LINE 2: Name the problem (in their language, not yours)
```

**Word count: 80–120 words maximum. Subject line: 5 words or fewer.**

### The Follow-Up Sequence — How Top SDRs Never Lose Leads

Most salespeople give up after 1–2 touches. The data (Gong, HubSpot, Yesware) consistently shows:

- ~2% of sales are made on the 1st contact
- ~5% on the 2nd contact
- ~10% on the 3rd contact
- **~80% of sales are made on the 5th–12th contact**

Yet 44% of salespeople give up after one follow-up.

**Your 8-touch sequence (over 21 days):**

| Day | Channel | Content |
|---|---|---|
| Day 1 | Email | Template 1 or 2 (insight-first) |
| Day 3 | LinkedIn | Connect or engage with their post |
| Day 5 | Email | Follow-up — add new data point or industry news peg |
| Day 8 | LinkedIn | Send DM — reply to something they posted |
| Day 11 | Email | New angle — share a relevant case study or 2-min Loom |
| Day 14 | LinkedIn | Comment on their company page's latest post |
| Day 17 | Email | The "breakup" email (see below) |
| Day 21 | LinkedIn | Final message |

**The "Breakup" Email (day 17):**
This counterintuitively gets a high reply rate because it respects the person:

> **Subject:** closing your file
>
> Hi [Name],
>
> I've reached out a few times and haven't heard back — completely understand, you're busy.
>
> I'll close out your file and won't contact you again. If timing is ever different or the challenge around customer support resolution becomes more pressing, feel free to reach back out.
>
> For what it's worth — I think what we've built is genuinely relevant to where [Company] is heading. No pressure at all.
>
> [Name]

**Why this works:** It removes pressure, acknowledges reality, and demonstrates confidence. Ironically, this gets some of the highest reply rates in the entire sequence — often "actually, let's chat."

# PART 5: THE DISCOVERY CALL
## How to Turn a 20-Minute Call Into a Committed Next Step

The discovery call is not a demo. Repeat that.

**A discovery call is a structured conversation where you earn the right to give a demo.**

Top performers at Salesforce and HubSpot follow the **MEDDIC framework** to qualify every prospect:

---

### MEDDIC — The Enterprise Qualification Framework

| Letter | What It Stands For | Question to Ask |
|---|---|---|
| **M** | Metrics — quantify the pain | "What does this problem cost you today — in time, in churn, in headcount?" |
| **E** | Economic Buyer — who controls the money | "Who else is involved in decisions like this — does it go through a CFO or CEO?" |
| **D** | Decision Criteria — how they choose | "What would need to be true for you to choose a new tool?" |
| **D** | Decision Process — how they buy | "What does your procurement process look like — do you do a trial, or go straight to POC?" |
| **I** | Identify Pain — the core problem | "What's the most expensive consequence of your current support setup?" |
| **C** | Champion — your internal seller | "Who else on your team cares most about this getting solved?" |

**You cannot close a deal if you are missing even one of these.** Top salespeople do not proceed to demo without qualifying all six.

---

### Discovery Call Structure (20–30 minutes)

**Minutes 0–2: Set the agenda**
> "Thanks for making time. I have about 20 minutes. My goal for today isn't to pitch you anything — I want to understand how your support team operates and see if we're even relevant to each other. If we're not a fit, I'll tell you honestly. Sound good?"

This sets a tone of no pressure and builds trust immediately.

**Minutes 2–10: Current State (Situation + Problem)**
Ask SPIN questions. Map their current state. Understand their tool, their team size, their ticket volume, their escalation rate, their CSAT, their biggest pain point.

**Minutes 10–18: Future State + Implication**
Ask Implication questions (the expensive part). Then Need-Payoff questions. Get them to describe the solution in their own words.

**Minutes 18–22: Proof of Concept**
Only now do you briefly say what Lira does — and you frame it entirely through the gap they just described:
> "What you've described — needing the AI to actually resolve the ticket rather than deflect it, with a memory of the customer's history — that's specifically what we built Lira for. Let me show you one specific scenario relevant to your business."

**Minutes 22–28: Next Step**
**Never end a call without a committed next step.** Not "I'll send you some info." A specific time:
> "Based on what you've shared, I think the most useful next step is a 30-minute demo where I walk you through exactly how Lira would handle your KYC hold scenario. Would [specific date and time] work?"

# PART 7: OBJECTION HANDLING
## What the Best Salespeople Do When Prospects Push Back

Great salespeople do not overcome objections. They **examine** them. Every objection is a question in disguise.

| Objection | What They're Really Saying | Response |
|---|---|---|
| *"We're happy with Intercom"* | "I don't see enough difference to justify the switch effort" | "Completely fair. Can I ask — does Intercom currently resolve tickets fully without a human opening them, or does it triage and direct? Because that's our specific differentiation." |
| *"It's too expensive"* | "I don't yet see the ROI clearly enough" | "Let me be direct about the math. You're paying $X/month for Intercom and $Y in support agent salary to handle what AI could handle. Lira at outcome-based pricing means you pay nothing for unresolved tickets. What would you need to see to confirm the math works?" |
| *"We're not ready"* | "I have other priorities right now" | "That makes sense. What would need to change for this to move up the priority list? Is it a specific milestone — like reaching 500 customers — or more about what you'd need to see from an AI tool?" |
| *"We need to evaluate more options"* | "I haven't seen enough to feel confident" | "Totally reasonable. Who else are you looking at? I ask because there are specific things Lira does that the alternatives genuinely don't — I'd rather tell you honestly where we win and where we don't than oversell." |
| *"Can you send me some info?"* | "I'm not ready to commit time yet" | "Of course. Let me send you our 3-minute product overview and a specific case study for [their use case]. I'll follow up in a week — would that timing work?" — Then set the follow-up call in the email you send. |
| *"We'd need to speak to our IT/security team"* | "We have internal process" | "Smart move. What are their typical requirements? We're built on AWS infrastructure, SOC 2 compliant — I can get you a security overview. Should we schedule a call that includes them?" |

# PART 8: THE CLOSE
## Lessons from the Best Closers in History

### What Closing Actually Is

Grant Cardone's core insight: **"The close begins at the start of the conversation — not at the end."**

Every step of the sales process is a micro-close:
- Getting the discovery call booked = close 1
- Getting them to admit the pain = close 2
- Getting them to agree on the future state = close 3
- Getting the demo booked = close 4
- Getting them to agree Lira solves their gap = close 5
- Getting budget confirmed = close 6
- Getting the contract signed = close 7

If you've done every micro-close correctly, the final "close" is just confirming paperwork.

---

### The 3 Closes That Actually Work in B2B SaaS

**Close 1: The Assumptive Close**
> "Based on what you've told me, I think the right starting point for [Company] is our Growth plan — it covers your volume and includes the proactive support module you said matters most. The next step is a 2-week pilot. Should I prepare that agreement?"

You are not asking if they want to buy. You are proposing the logical next step.

**Close 2: The Summary Close**
> "Let me summarize what I heard: you're currently spending $2,800/month on Intercom and one full-time support agent, your escalation rate is 40%, and your CSAT is dropping. Lira's outcome-based model at your volume would cost you approximately $800/month, your escalation rate goes to sub-20%, and you pay nothing for unresolved tickets. The ROI case is there. What's your biggest concern about moving forward?"

List the value. Surface the last real objection. Solve it. Ask for next step.

**Close 3: The Alternative Close**
> "We can either start with a 2-week pilot on your customer support channel only — low risk, zero commitment — or go to a full onboarding across email, chat, and voice. Which makes more sense given where you are right now?"

You are not asking if they want to proceed. You are asking how they want to proceed. The sale is assumed.

---

### What to Do When Someone Goes Silent

The #1 deal killer in B2B is not rejection — it is silence. Prospects disappear.

**Chris Voss's Rule** (from *Never Split the Difference* — FBI hostage negotiator turned negotiation coach):

When someone goes dark, send this email:
> **Subject:** have you given up on this?
>
> Hi [Name] — have you given up on solving the support resolution problem we talked about?
>
> [Name]

That's the entire email. Nothing else. This message triggers a response because it implies a yes/no choice that most people feel compelled to correct. Response rate on this format: north of 60% in most email sequences.

# APPENDIX: Lessons from History's Greatest Salespeople

### Zig Ziglar
> *"You can have everything in life you want if you will just help enough other people get what they want."*
Sales is not manipulation. It is alignment. When what you sell genuinely solves what they need, the close is just confirming math.

### Aaron Ross
> *"Specialization — separating your prospecting motion, your closing motion, and your account management motion — is the single biggest lever in scaling from $1M to $10M ARR."*
Even as a small team: the person writing cold emails should not be the same person running demos. Specialize before you need to.

### David Sandler
> *"You don't have to like prospecting. You just have to do it."*
Consistency beats genius. The SDR who sends 20 emails per day for 90 days will outperform the brilliant seller who sends 3 perfect emails per week.

### Neil Rackham
> *"In major sales, closing techniques can actually reduce your chances of getting a sale."*
In B2B, pressure closes destroy trust. Implication questions do more closing work than any technique.

### Keenan
> *"Selling is not about products; it is about identifying problems, understanding the gap, and creating a path to the desired future state."*
If you can't articulate the gap better than your prospect can, you haven't done the research.

### Grant Cardone
> *"The number one reason people fail in sales is that they don't make enough contacts."*
Volume matters. 10 thoughtful emails is not enough. 100 thoughtful emails, personalized to each segment, at consistent cadence — that is how you build pipeline.

### Chris Voss
> *"The fastest way to get someone to trust you is to prove you understand the way they see the world before you say anything about yourself."*
Empathy is your most powerful sales tool. "Tell me what's making this difficult for your team right now" is more powerful than any product pitch.

## One-Page Cheat Sheet — The Lira Sales Motion

```
WHO: Head of CX / VP Customer Success at 20–500 person BFSI company
PAIN: Reactive support, high escalation rate, Intercom not resolving tickets
INSIGHT: "Your support cost is $8 per ticket. AI costs $0.10. 
          But only if the AI actually resolves — most don't."
GAP: Current state (reactive, expensive, high escalation) → 
     Future state (proactive, autonomous, CSAT improving)
OPEN: Insight-first cold email (80 words max)
QUALIFY: SPIN questions on discovery call → MEDDIC checklist
DEMO: One scenario — their specific industry pain — the hero story
CLOSE: Assumptive next step — 2-week pilot
FOLLOW UP: 8-touch sequence, 21 days
CONVERT: Champion your first 3 customers into case studies
EXPAND: Partner channel once proof exists
```
